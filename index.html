Safety — read first
No water at North Rim or Supai Tunnel
Confirmed by NPS as of June 3, 2026. First potable water is Manzanita Rest Area (~3 mi in). Carry minimum 2L per person from your hotel before boarding the shuttle. Do not count on anything at the trailhead.
Elevated flash flood risk — Dragon Bravo burn scar
July is peak monsoon season. The 2025 wildfire burn scar dramatically accelerates runoff along the upper North Kaibab — a storm you can't see can send debris and water down the drainage with almost no warning. Register for GCRIVERALERTS before you go. If storms are forecast on the Kaibab Plateau the morning of your hike, shelter at Cottonwood and wait.
Rockfall and widow-maker hazard — upper North Kaibab
The burn scar between the trailhead and Supai Tunnel (~first 1.7 mi) has active rockfall and falling tree hazard. NPS guidance: move steadily and limit breaks in this zone. Do not stop under standing dead trees. Avoid the section during high winds.
Silver Bridge and River Trail — verify before July 18
Current NPS closure runs through June 30, 2026. If it reopens on schedule, your Bright Angel exit is unaffected. If the closure slips again, your only exit from Phantom Ranch to the South Rim is South Kaibab Trail — steeper, no water, no shade, 7 miles on tired legs.
Cottonwood — use the campground tap, not the day use area
NPS confirmed the day use area tap at Cottonwood is off. Walk to the campground side to refill. Water statuses across all stations can change suddenly due to pipeline breaks — always carry your filter.
North Kaibab Trail fully open
Entire trail from North Rim trailhead to Phantom Ranch confirmed open as of May 15, 2026. Foot traffic only; stock use suspended for the season. Expect possible brief delays near maintenance crews.
Pipeline breaks — uneven trail surface near Phantom Ranch
Recent Transcanyon pipeline breaks have created upheaved trail sections and water bubblers near Phantom Ranch. Relevant on Day 2 — you're departing at 3am by headlamp. Watch your footing on the trail between Phantom Ranch and Black Bridge.
Flash flood risk at Phantom Ranch — not just the upper canyon
NPS specifically flags that flash flooding can occur along the lower North Kaibab Trail and in the Phantom Ranch area with little to no warning, even when storms are not visible overhead. Stay alert to posted forecasts and instructions from park staff. Be prepared to move to higher ground immediately if an evacuation is issued.
Bright Angel Trail water confirmed on
Havasupai Gardens, 3-Mile Resthouse, 1.5-Mile Resthouse, and Bright Angel Trailhead all confirmed on as of June 3, 2026. Day 2 is well-supplied.

The core rule
Every time you drink, you also eat or take electrolytes
Hyponatremia — dangerously low blood sodium from drinking too much plain water — kills canyon hikers and looks identical to dehydration. Never drink plain water exclusively for more than one consecutive stop. Alternate electrolyte water and plain water.
Hydration protocol
While moving
Drink 0.5L every 30–45 min. Sip continuously — never chug. At each water source drink 0.5–1L on the spot before refilling bottles.
Electrolyte timing
Supplement every 60–90 min while moving. Carry 12 total servings across both days.
Best pick — heavy sweaters
LMNT — highest sodium of any mainstream product. Single-serve stick packs. This is the call for someone who sweats a lot.
Backup / alternate
Nuun Sport tabs — dissolve in a bottle, light to carry. 1–2 tubes.
Appetite suppressed
Liquid IV — adds carbohydrates alongside electrolytes when you can't stomach solid food in the heat.
Day 0 pre-loading
Electrolytes in 2–3 water servings throughout the day before — spread across the whole day, not just the evening.

The NPS recommends eating double your normal caloric intake for summer canyon hikes. Calories play a direct role in regulating body temperature.

How to use this schedule
Eat on a clock, not on hunger
Heat kills appetite well before it kills performance. By the time you feel hungry in the canyon in July, you're already behind. Use this schedule as a timer — eat something at every stop regardless of how you feel.
Every eating stop is also an electrolyte stop
Never eat dry salty snacks with plain water. Every food stop pairs with either an electrolyte drink (LMNT or Nuun in your bottle) or a gel that contains sodium. This is how you prevent both bonking and hyponatremia simultaneously.

Know these before you start

Ranger stations with emergency capability: Cottonwood Camp, Phantom Ranch, Havasupai Gardens, 1.5-Mile Resthouse, 3-Mile Resthouse.

Heat exhaustion
Warning level — act now before it becomes a medical emergency
Signs: heavy sweating, weakness, cold or clammy skin, nausea, headache, dizziness
Action: Stop immediately. Get in shade. Drink electrolytes. Wet the skin. Rest until you feel genuinely better — this can take an hour or more.
Do not: Push through. This is the last warning your body gives before heat stroke.
Heat stroke
Medical emergency — call for help immediately
Signs: hot, red, dry skin; confusion; rapid pulse; loss of coherence or responsiveness
Action: Stop moving immediately. Cool the body aggressively with water. Send someone for help — do not leave the person alone. Ranger stations are at Cottonwood, Phantom Ranch, Havasupai Gardens, 1.5-Mile and 3-Mile Resthouses.
A helicopter rescue costs $10,000+ and puts rescuers at risk. Heat stroke is preventable.
Hyponatremia
Too much plain water, not enough salt — looks like dehydration
Signs: headache, nausea, confusion, swelling in hands or feet — mimics dehydration exactly
Action: Stop drinking water. Eat salty food immediately. Rest in shade.
Prevention: Electrolytes every 60–90 min while hiking. Salty snacks at every stop. Never drink plain water exclusively for more than one stop in a row.
Flash flood
Elevated risk in 2026 — Dragon Bravo burn scar
Risk: Can occur with no visible storm overhead. The burned watershed channels water with almost no absorption — a distant storm means fast-rising water in the drainage.
Signs: Roaring noise upstream, sudden rise in creek color or volume, smell of mud
Action: Move to high ground immediately. Face upstream when near the creek. Do not re-enter the drainage until the all-clear. Register for GCRIVERALERTS before your trip.
The rule neither of you can break
If one person says stop, you both stop
Agree on this before you start. Canyon fatalities routinely involve someone who felt off but kept moving because they didn't want to slow down their partner. The canyon will still be there. If either of you shows signs of heat illness and does not recover within an hour of rest, shade, and electrolytes — call for help rather than push on.

Water — highest priority
  • Sawyer Squeeze water filter
    Screws directly onto a standard Smartwater bottle — no dedicated bladder needed. Backflush with the included syringe before the trip if bought ahead of time. ~$30–35 at REI or Amazon.
  • 2x 1L Smartwater bottles per person
    Pairs perfectly with the Sawyer Squeeze — threads fit directly. Use one as your dirty-water collection vessel for filtering creek water in The Box. Combined with a hydration bladder, target 3–4L total carry capacity per person.
Electrolytes and nutrition
  • LMNT electrolyte packs — 12 total for both days
    Best option for heavy sweaters. Highest sodium of any mainstream product.
  • Nuun Sport tablets — 1–2 tubes as backup
    Light, dissolvable, easy to carry as a secondary option.
  • Energy gels — 8 total (Gu or Maurten)
    For The Box and upper Bright Angel when solid food feels impossible in the heat.
  • Salty carry food: pretzels, salted nuts, Chomps, tortillas + PB
    Sodium is as important as calories. Prioritize salty over sweet.
Cooling and clothing
  • Compact UPF 50 umbrella (Repel or Chrome Dome)
    Cuts radiant heat dramatically in The Box. Sounds odd, genuinely essential in 110°F canyon corridor. ~11 oz.
  • Buff / neck gaiter — 1 per person
    Wet at every water source. Evaporative cooling lasts 20–30 min per soak.
  • Sunscreen SPF 50+
    Apply before you start even in the dark. The sun hits exposed upper trail sections fast once it clears the canyon walls.
Stability and comfort
  • Trekking poles — collapsible carbon fiber (Black Diamond or LEKI)
    5,760 ft of descent on Day 1 will destroy your quads and knees without poles. Not optional.
  • Leukotape or moleskin for blisters
    Leukotape stays on in sweaty conditions far better than moleskin. Apply at the first hint of hot spots, not after.
  • Ibuprofen and acetaminophen
    Alternate for joint and muscle pain. Both in an accessible pocket, not buried in the pack.
Safety
  • Emergency whistle — clipped to shoulder strap
    Weighs nothing. Carries further than a voice in canyon acoustics.
  • 1 headlamp per person + 1 shared backup
    Fresh batteries or fully charged the night before. You are starting both days in complete darkness.
